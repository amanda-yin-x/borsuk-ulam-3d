The Borsuk–Ulam Theorem

§ 01 · Overview

A promise about continuous maps.

Right now on Earth there are two points on exactly opposite sides of the planet that have the same temperature and the same air pressure. This is not a coincidence. It is a theorem.

Pick any two quantities you can measure continuously across the entire surface of a sphere. Temperature and pressure. Altitude and humidity. Whatever you want. The theorem says there will always be some point whose exact opposite has matching values. Down to the last digit. No matter how chaotic the data looks.

Stanisław Ulam conjectured it around 1930 during late-night discussions at the Scottish Café in Lwów. Karol Borsuk proved it three years later in his paper Drei Sätze über die n-dimensionale euklidische Sphäre, published in Fundamenta Mathematicae in 1933.[1]

What makes it beautiful is how little it assumes. The only requirement is continuity. No smoothness, no symmetry, no hidden structure. Just the basic rule that nearby points have nearby values. From that alone, you get an exact match. It feels like magic but it's really topology.

“The Borsuk–Ulam theorem is a wonderful piece of topology: a precisely stated, geometrically vivid result whose proof requires real mathematical depth, but whose consequences reach into combinatorics, fair division, and even computer science.” Jiří Matoušek, Using the Borsuk–Ulam Theorem (2003)

§ 02 · The theorem

Three ways to say the same thing.

The theorem can be phrased in a few different ways. All three versions below are equivalent. Each one highlights a different angle on the same idea.

Coincidence form. Let $f \colon S^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be continuous. Then there exists $x \in S^n$ such that $f(x) = f(-x)$.

Let $g \colon S^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be a continuous odd map, meaning $g(-x) = -g(x)$. Then $g(x_0) = 0$ for some $x_0 \in S^n$.

There is no continuous odd map $h \colon S^n \to S^{n-1}$. In plainer words: you can't squish a sphere onto a smaller sphere while keeping opposite points opposite.

Moving between these versions is not hard. Normalize $f(x) - f(-x)$ and you get an odd sphere-to-sphere map. Treat the vanishing of an odd map as the obstruction and you get version 2. For the visualization that follows, the coincidence form is the one to keep in mind. It's the one with the Earth story.

Why the dimension matters
$n$ outputs fail · $n + 1$ succeed

The theorem is tight. Map $S^n$ into $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ (one more output coordinate) and the result breaks. Here's the counterexample:

$S^n \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^{n+1}, \quad x \mapsto x$

Just embed the sphere into Euclidean space as itself. That's continuous, and clearly no two opposite points land on the same spot (they're opposite).

So the theorem is saying something precise: $n$ continuous outputs per point is just barely not enough to separate all the antipodal pairs. With $n+1$ outputs you can. That gap of one dimension is what the theorem is really about.

§ 02 · continued

Why the $n = 2$ case is the interesting one.

Earth's surface is a 2-sphere. Temperature and pressure are two continuous functions on it. Together they make up a map $f \colon S^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ where $f(x) = (T(x),\, P(x))$. Apply the theorem with $n = 2$ and you get a point $x^\star$ where $T(x^\star) = T(-x^\star)$ and $P(x^\star) = P(-x^\star)$ at the same time.

The word and is doing all the work here. Matching one equation on its own is not hard. Walk around any great circle measuring temperature, apply the Intermediate Value Theorem, done. Same story for pressure. But Borsuk–Ulam promises something much stronger: you can find one single antipodal pair that satisfies both equations simultaneously. That joint condition is what no elementary 1D argument can provide.

The conclusion is a little eerie when you picture it. Right this second there is some spot on Earth whose exact opposite has the same temperature and the same pressure. Down to the last millibar. Every moment. Always.

§ 03 · The one-dimensional case

The proof you can do in your head.

The $n = 1$ case has a proof so clean it fits in one paragraph. Three ingredients: oddness, continuity, and the Intermediate Value Theorem.

Setup

Let $f \colon S^1 \to \mathbb{R}$ be any continuous function on the circle. Picture it as temperature measured as you walk once around the equator. We want to show there are two opposite points $x$ and $-x$ with $f(x) = f(-x)$.

The auxiliary function

Define

$g(x) \;=\; f(x) - f(-x)$

Since $f$ is continuous, so is $g$. Now watch what happens when you swap $x$ for $-x$:

$g(-x) \;=\; f(-x) - f(x) \;=\; -\,g(x)$

So $g$ is an odd function. Its value at any point equals the negative of its value at the opposite point. That is the only thing we need.

The IVT finish

Pick any starting point $p$ on the circle. One of three things must be true:

i

$g(p) = 0$

Then $p$ and $-p$ already have equal $f$-values. Done.

ii

$g(p) > 0$

Then $g(-p) = -g(p) < 0$.

iii

$g(p) < 0$

Then $g(-p) = -g(p) > 0$.

Cases ii and iii are the same story. $g$ is continuous and takes both positive and negative values somewhere on the circle. By the Intermediate Value Theorem, applied along either arc from $p$ to $-p$, there's a point $q$ in between where $g(q) = 0$. Meaning $f(q) = f(-q)$. That's our antipodal pair. ∎

Visual · the odd function $g$
Why $g$ has to cross zero
The higher-dimensional cases need much heavier tools because IVT only works in 1D. It can't force two equations to be zero simultaneously on a 2-sphere. But the core idea, turn “find a matching pair” into “find a zero of an odd map”, shows up everywhere.
§ 04 · Higher dimensions — a sketch

Ščepin's route up, in outline.

The higher-dimensional proof needs tools from algebraic topology (winding numbers, general position) beyond the scope of this project. What follows is the architecture of Ščepin's argument, not a proof from scratch.

A note on scope

The 1D proof in the previous tab is rigorous and uses only intro real analysis. The higher-dimensional cases genuinely require topology machinery, so we provide a sketch here. I sketch the structure here for context, drawing on Ščepin [3] and Matoušek [2] for the technical details.

Ščepin's paper[3] proves the higher-dimensional Borsuk–Ulam theorem by reducing it to the 2D case. If you already know the 2D version, the rest follows by induction. The 2D case itself has an elementary proof using combinatorial tools (Tucker's lemma) which still lies outside intro real analysis.

The strategy in five steps

1

Contrapositive.

Assume some continuous $f \colon S^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ sends no pair of opposite points to the same value. Now derive a contradiction.

2

Turn $f$ into an odd sphere map.

Define $g(x) = (f(x) - f(-x)) / \|f(x) - f(-x)\|$. The denominator never vanishes (our assumption), so $g$ is continuous, and it satisfies $g(-x) = -g(x)$. You now have a continuous odd map $S^n \to S^{n-1}$. This step is straightforward.

3

Drop a dimension (the key lemma).

Ščepin shows: for $n \geq 4$, any continuous odd map $S^n \to S^{n-1}$ gives rise to one $S^{n-1} \to S^{n-2}$. The construction uses a geometric object he calls a “simple section” and relies on general-position arguments from differential topology. The details are nontrivial and not reproduced here.

4

Handle $n = 3$ separately.

The reduction fails for $n \leq 3$ for a technical reason about needing enough ambient space. Ščepin handles $n = 3$ with its own argument that ultimately reaches down to the 2D case.

5

Hit bottom and contradict.

The chain of reductions eventually hands you a continuous odd map $S^2 \to S^1$. But no such map exists (this is the 2D base case, provable via winding numbers or Tucker's lemma). Contradiction. So our original $f$ couldn't exist either.

The ladder of reductions
Descending one dimension at a time
$S^n$ → odd map to $S^{n-1}$ by the key lemma
$S^{n-1}$ → odd map to $S^{n-2}$
⋮ keep descending
$S^3$ → odd map to $S^2$ (special n=3 argument)
$S^2$ → no odd map to $S^1$ — contradiction ∎

Each step cuts dimension by one. At the bottom, the 2D case (proved elementarily with Tucker's lemma) gives the contradiction we need.

What the key lemma does (intuition)
The geometric idea

Given an odd map $f\colon S^n \to S^{n-1}$, look at the preimages of two opposite “regular values.” For a generic value, each preimage is a 1-dimensional graph. Find a centrally symmetric hypersurface (a “simple section”) that separates them. That hypersurface is topologically $S^{n-1}$. Restrict $f$ to it, compose with the retraction $S^{n-1} \setminus \{\pm A\} \to S^{n-2}$, and you've got a dimension-reduced odd map. Full construction in [3].

§ 04 · continued

The two people behind the theorem.

Karol Borsuk
Warsaw · 1905–1982

Professor at the University of Warsaw and a central figure of the Warsaw School of Mathematics. Proved the theorem in 1933 as one of three results in his paper. During WWII he ran a secret stationery store that doubled as a Home Army meeting point and gave underground lectures. Later pioneered shape theory.

Stanisław Ulam
Lwów → Los Alamos · 1909–1984

Conjectured the theorem around 1930 at the Scottish Café in Lwów, where mathematicians (Banach, Mazur, and others) used to hang out and scribble problems into the Scottish Book. Later moved to the US, worked on the Manhattan Project, and invented Monte Carlo with von Neumann. His memoir Adventures of a Mathematician covers the Lwów years.

§ 05 · continued

The three acts of the animation.

Act 1 · Search (frames 1–240)
The spiral sweep

Two antipodal markers sweep a spiral path across the sphere. Four colored bars at the markers track the values of $T$ and $P$ at both points in real time. Most of the time, the bars don't match. That's normal.

Act 2 · Glide (frames 241–280)
The smooth transition

The markers smoothly glide from the end of the spiral search to the true matching pair, found by evaluating mismatch at 20,000 sampled points on the sphere. Bezier easing keeps the transition feeling natural.

Act 3 · Payoff (frames 281–380)
The four-second hold

Markers pulse white-gold. The diameter line flares warm gold. A text banner reading $f(x) = f(-x)$ appears. Four seconds of hold so the viewer can actually absorb what just happened. Then everything fades back to baseline.

§ 06 · Construction in Blender 4.5

From empty scene to finished visualization.

The scene is built as a sequence of nine conceptual steps. Each one has a specific mathematical job in telling the theorem's story.

Scene graph · how objects depend on each other
The single source of truth

One thing is animated: the AntipodeDriver. Everything else follows automatically. Markers read its position through Blender's driver system. The diameter tracks Marker A via a Damped Track constraint. This is how we guarantee the markers stay mathematically antipodal at every single frame.

1

The sphere.

A UV sphere at the origin with smooth shading, representing the domain $S^2$. Rotates slowly on its vertical axis. The rotation has no mathematical meaning. It just lets the camera see different parts of the surface as the animation plays.

Mesh → UV Sphere Shade Smooth Rotation keyframes
2

Two continuous fields on the surface.

The map $f \colon S^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ is painted onto the sphere with a procedural shader built from two Perlin-noise textures. One goes through a color ramp mapping to a cool blue → cream → warm terracotta gradient (“temperature”). The other subtly modulates brightness (“pressure”). Both noises use 3D object coordinates so the pattern is locked to the sphere.

Shader Editor Noise Texture ×2 Color Ramp Mix (Multiply) Principled BSDF
3

A driver empty as the single source of truth.

An invisible Empty object sits at the origin. Its animated position represents the axis pointing through the antipodal pair. One direction toward Marker A, the negative toward Marker B. This is the only thing we animate. Everything else is reactive.

Add → Empty Named "AntipodeDriver"
4

Two markers, mirrored via drivers.

Two emissive spheres (one terracotta, one blue). Each of their X, Y, Z position components is controlled by a Driver reading the Empty's corresponding coordinate times $\pm 1.02$. Because both markers read the same source, they stay mathematically antipodal at every frame.

UV Sphere ×2 Emission shader Scripted Driver ×6
5

A diameter line via Damped Track.

A thin cylinder at the origin plays the diameter. A Damped Track constraint tells it to always point its $+Z$ axis at Marker A. Since Marker B is opposite, the cylinder's other end tracks Marker B for free.

Cylinder Damped Track constraint
6

Four value bars keyframed per frame.

At each marker, two thin emissive bars sit radially. A terracotta bar for $f_1$ (temperature), a blue bar for $f_2$ (pressure). Heights are scaled to the magnitude of the respective function evaluated at the marker's position. Orientations rotate radially outward using spherical-to-Euler conversion. Location, scale, and rotation are keyframed every frame.

Cylinder ×4 Emission Per-frame keyframes
7

Global search for the true match.

The spiral sweep covers a curve on the sphere, not the full 2D surface. To find the globally correct matching pair, 20,000 uniformly distributed points (via Fibonacci lattice) are evaluated under the mismatch function $M(x) = |f_1(x) - f_1(-x)| + |f_2(x) - f_2(-x)|$. The minimum is found and pinned as the animation's target.

Fibonacci lattice Mismatch minimization Baked as keyframe
8

Glide, freeze, and celebrate.

Frames 241–280 interpolate the AntipodeDriver smoothly from spiral-end to the true match. Frames 281–380 hold on the match for four seconds. Markers' emission materials pulse up from baseline color to bright white-gold and back. The diameter flares warm gold. A 3D text object reading $f(x) = f(-x)$ fades in via scale keyframes.

Bezier easing Material keyframes Text object Track To (camera)
9

Lighting, camera, render.

Three area lights in a classic three-point arrangement (key, fill, back), each with a Track To constraint pointing at the sphere. Camera half-orbits the scene over 10 seconds with a Track To so the sphere stays framed. Final render is Eevee Next at 1920×1080 / 24fps / Standard color management.
